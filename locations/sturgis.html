Sturgis Homeowners Face Serious Radon Risk Year-Round

Sturgis, South Dakota is world-famous for the Sturgis Motorcycle Rally, which draws hundreds of thousands of visitors to this Meade County community every August. But for the approximately 7,000 residents who call Sturgis home year-round, there is a less visible concern that demands attention: dangerously elevated indoor radon levels. Situated in the northern foothills of the Black Hills along the Bear Butte Creek valley, Sturgis sits on geological formations that produce significant quantities of radon gas — making professional testing and mitigation essential for every home in the community.

The EPA classifies Meade County as Zone 1 for radon risk, with predicted average indoor levels exceeding 4 pCi/L. The soils surrounding Sturgis are derived from the weathering of sedimentary rocks, including the Minnelusa Formation and Spearfish Formation, which contain trace amounts of uranium. As this uranium undergoes radioactive decay, radon gas is released and migrates through porous soils and fractured rock into the crawlspaces and basements of residential structures. Bear Butte, the prominent geological landmark just northeast of town, is a testament to the volcanic and igneous activity that has enriched the region's soils with radioactive minerals.

Rally Town, Real Risks — Why Sturgis Homes Need Radon Testing

While the Sturgis Rally brings international attention and economic vitality, the everyday reality for year-round residents includes the same radon exposure risks faced throughout western South Dakota. Many homes in Sturgis were built in the mid-20th century with full basements constructed from poured concrete or concrete block. These older foundations often have deteriorated mortar joints, settling cracks, and unsealed floor-wall junctions that serve as direct conduits for radon gas. Even the historic homes along Junction Avenue and the well-maintained neighborhoods near Sturgis Brown High School regularly test above the EPA action level.

Newer developments on the outskirts of Sturgis, including areas along Otter Road, Blucksberg Mountain, and the Fort Meade VA property, are not immune either. Radon potential is determined by geology, not construction age. Without an active sub-slab depressurization system, any home built on Meade County soil is likely to accumulate radon to levels that increase the risk of lung cancer with prolonged exposure.
