Your Test Came Back High. Now What?

You had your home tested — maybe for a real estate transaction, maybe just to be safe — and the number came back above 4.0 pCi/L. Now you're staring at a report that says your family has been breathing elevated radon and you're not sure what to do next.

First: take a breath. You did the right thing by testing.

Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in the United States, responsible for about 21,000 deaths per year according to the EPA. It's a naturally occurring radioactive gas that seeps from the soil into homes through foundation cracks, slab joints, and utility penetrations. It's odorless, colorless, and completely invisible — which is exactly why most people don't know they have a problem until they test.

Rapid City sits in EPA Radon Zone 1 — the highest-risk designation. The Black Hills granite geology beneath Rapid City produces some of the highest radon concentrations in South Dakota, which already leads the nation in average indoor radon levels. The EPA action level is 4.0 pCi/L. The average U.S. home tests at 1.3 pCi/L. If your result is above 4.0, the EPA recommends mitigation. If it's above 8.0, they recommend acting quickly.

Here's the part most people don't know: high radon is not a death sentence for your home. It is completely fixable.

What Happens If You Wait

The instinct after a high radon test is to do more research, get a second opinion, wait and see. That's understandable — it's an invisible threat, and it's hard to feel urgency about something you can't smell or see.

But radon exposure is cumulative. Every month you delay mitigation, your family continues to breathe elevated levels. And radon risk compounds over time — it's not a single exposure event, it's years of accumulated lung tissue exposure to radioactive particles.

  • "Maybe the test was wrong." Short-term tests can vary. If your result is above 4.0, the EPA recommends confirming with a long-term test — but if it's significantly above 4.0 (say, 8.0 or higher), mitigation is the right call. A good contractor will tell you this honestly.
  • "Will the system be ugly or loud?" Radon mitigation systems are more discreet than most homeowners expect. A pipe runs through the home to a small exterior fan, typically mounted on the side of the house or in a utility area. Most homeowners barely notice them after installation.
  • "What if it doesn't work?" Reputable mitigation contractors guarantee their results. If post-mitigation testing shows levels still above 4.0 pCi/L, they come back and fix it. A written guarantee is standard.
  • "Is my family already harmed?" Radon risk is real, and prolonged high-level exposure increases lung cancer risk — especially in smokers. But most elevated radon situations are caught in time. The best thing you can do right now is mitigate and eliminate the ongoing exposure.

Radon Risk in Rapid City — What You Should Know

Rapid City sits in EPA Radon Zone 1 — the highest-risk designation. The Black Hills granite geology beneath Rapid City produces some of the highest radon concentrations in South Dakota, which already leads the nation in average indoor radon levels. The uranium-bearing granite and metamorphic rocks that give the Black Hills their dramatic landscape also produce significant radon gas as uranium decays naturally in the soil and bedrock. That gas migrates upward through foundation cracks and into the air inside your home — where it accumulates without any visible sign.

South Dakota's cold winters compound the risk. When homeowners seal their houses against the weather, radon levels rise because there's less ventilation to dilute the gas. Neighborhoods like Robbinsdale and Canyon Lake sit on the same geological base as the rest of Pennington County — and the same elevated radon potential applies. Many Rapid City families spend the most time indoors during the very months when radon concentrations are highest.
