When Does a Radon Fan Need Replacement?

The radon fan is the heart of your mitigation system. It runs continuously, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, creating the negative pressure beneath your foundation that keeps radon gas from entering your home. Like any mechanical device that operates non-stop, radon fans eventually wear out. Most quality radon fans have a service life of 8 to 12 years, though some may fail sooner due to environmental conditions, manufacturing defects, or installation factors. When your fan stops working, your radon mitigation system stops protecting you.

Many homeowners in Rapid City do not realize their radon fan has failed until they notice the U-tube manometer on their system piping showing equal liquid levels (no pressure differential) or until a follow-up radon test reveals elevated levels. Because radon is invisible and odorless, a fan failure gives no obvious warning signs inside your home. That is why it is important to check your manometer regularly and to recognize the early warning signs that your fan may be approaching the end of its life.

If your existing radon mitigation system was installed by another company, or if it came with the home when you purchased it, we can replace the fan regardless of who installed the original system. We service all brands and models and can often upgrade your system with a more efficient, quieter replacement fan that delivers better performance than the original.

Signs Your Radon Fan Is Failing

Watch for these warning indicators that your radon fan may need replacement:

  • U-tube manometer shows equal levels — When the liquid in both sides of the manometer is at the same height, the fan is no longer creating suction. This is the clearest indicator of fan failure.
  • Unusual noise or vibration — A radon fan that begins making grinding, rattling, or buzzing sounds is experiencing bearing wear. This noise will worsen over time and the fan will eventually fail completely.
  • Intermittent operation — A fan that cycles on and off, or that starts only after a delay, is showing signs of motor degradation. Even intermittent operation means your home is unprotected during off periods.
  • Excessive heat from the fan housing — If the fan motor housing is hot to the touch, the motor is working harder than it should, typically due to worn bearings or internal damage.
  • Rising radon test results — If a follow-up radon test shows higher levels than your post-mitigation baseline, the fan may be losing suction capacity even though it is still running.
  • Fan is more than 10 years old — Even if your fan appears to be working, fans older than 10 years are approaching end-of-life. Proactive replacement prevents unexpected failures.
